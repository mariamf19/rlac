For this response I gave two of my Assignment 1 visualizations to Claude with no context, then iteratively added information to see how the interpretation changed. The two visuals were the R heatmap comparing word frequencies across five texts and the Voyant Trends chart tracking romantic vocabulary across the same corpus. What the experiment showed me is that visuals are not as context-dependent as I expected, but they are more interpretation-dependent than I would have guessed — and those are not the same thing.

## Round 1: What the LLM Saw Without Being Told Anything

When I uploaded the heatmap with no context, Claude identified it correctly as a word frequency heatmap, named all five texts from the truncated filenames, and noticed the two most significant patterns: the dominance of "death" and "dark" in Deathly Hallows, and the elevated "kiss" count in yellowsunchild's fanfic. It even offered an interpretation — "which tracks with how fanfic tends to emphasize romance."

![LLM response to heatmap with no context](/rlac/assets/images/posts/llm-round1.png)
*Round 1: Claude's response to the heatmap with zero context provided.*

This surprised me. The filenames were doing a lot of work, but the interpretive move — connecting the kiss count to fanfic conventions — came from background knowledge about the texts, not from reading the axis labels. This is what Kestemont and Herman note in "Can Computers Read Literature?": computational analysis is never purely formal. The analyst always brings prior knowledge to the interpretation. So did the LLM, even without being asked to.

## Round 2: The Same Word, Two Different Meanings

The more revealing test was the romance vocabulary chart. Claude correctly identified it as a Voyant output and immediately flagged the dominant pattern: "eyes* dominates every single corpus." It interpreted yellowsunchild's spike as romantic gaze — "the grammar of slow-burn romance," it said.

![LLM response to romance chart with no context](/rlac/assets/images/posts/llm-round2.png)
*Round 2: Claude's response to the Voyant romance chart with zero context.*

That interpretation is correct for yellowsunchild. In Deathly Hallows, which also has a high "eyes*" count, it is wrong. Those eyes belong to Voldemort. Characters are tracking threats across a battlefield. The chart cannot show that difference. Without context, the same word at the same frequency produced two completely different readings — and the LLM had no way to know which one applied.

This is the core argument in Ted Underwood's chapter on the risks of distant reading: frequency data is not self-interpreting, and the most dangerous moment in a distant reading project is when a pattern seems obvious. The "eyes*" spike looked like it meant romance. In Deathly Hallows it meant war.

## Round 3: What Changed With Context

When I explained the corpus and the research question, the interpretation shifted immediately. Claude reread the Deathly Hallows "eyes*" count as surveillance and threat rather than intimacy, and offered a distinction I had not articulated clearly myself: "Rowling uses eyes descriptively. The fanfic writers are using them affectively." It also identified that the fanfics show a more even spread of romantic vocabulary while Rowling's terms are flattened under the eyes* spike.

![LLM response after context was provided](/rlac/assets/images/posts/llm-round3.png)
*Round 3: The same chart, reinterpreted after the corpus and research question were explained.*

What the LLM needed was not just labels and axis titles. It needed the research question and the contextual knowledge about the texts. The visualization is a starting point, not a conclusion. As the Distant Reading podcast with Ama Bemma Adwetewa-Badu describes, the interpretive work that computational methods cannot replace is exactly this: deciding what a frequency means, not just reading what it is.

## Round 4: Where the Argument Holds and Where It Does Not

In the final round I asked Claude to evaluate my central claim — that Rowling wrote a war story and fanfiction turned it into a love story — and tell me where the data supports it and where it does not. It confirmed that "touch*" and "kiss*" are the strongest evidence, since those words require physical intimacy to be present in the narrative. But it pushed back in two ways: first, that frequency cannot tell you function — "love" used philosophically and "love" used romantically are counted identically; second, that my fanfic sample is romance-coded on AO3, so I am measuring what explicitly romantic fanfiction does, not what fanfiction in general does.

![LLM evaluating the central argument](/rlac/assets/images/posts/llm-round4.png)
*Round 4: Claude evaluating the domestication thesis and flagging its limits.*

Its final point was: "You're using the corpus as a map, not as proof." That is the right framing, and I could not have articulated it as cleanly without the prompting process this assignment required.

## What This Means

Visuals do not speak on their own, but they are not mute either. What they cannot do is interpret correctly without the research question and contextual knowledge that surround them. The wrong reading in Round 2 pointed directly at the part of my argument that needed more careful handling. That is the most useful thing the exercise produced — not confirmation that my charts were clear, but a precise map of where they were not.
